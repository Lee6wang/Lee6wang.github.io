# Interrupts and RTOS: Rules, Boundaries, and Engineering Consequences

**- Why "casually adding one line in an ISR" can become the seed of a system crash**

> In many FreeRTOS projects, interrupt code often evolves step by step like this:
>
> At first it is very restrained and does only the most basic work. Later, as requirements grow, people start to "do a little more" inside the interrupt. Later still, the ISR contains branches, logic, synchronization, and even log printing.
>
> The system does not fail immediately.
>
> But during some upgrade, some stress test, or some completely unexpected moment, the system suddenly locks up, HardFaults, or produces behavior that can no longer be reproduced. The problem appears to have no pattern, **but the root cause is almost always the same: you crossed the invisible boundary between the RTOS and interrupts.**

This article does not teach "how to write an interrupt." It answers a more important question:

> **In the world of an RTOS, what should an interrupt actually do, and what must it absolutely not do?**

---

## 1. A Fact That Must Be Made Clear First

In FreeRTOS, there is a fact that is often ignored but is extremely important:

> **Interrupts do not belong to the RTOS scheduling system.**

This is the starting point of almost every interrupt-related problem.

**The world of tasks** runs inside the "order" of the RTOS: tasks have explicit priorities, states such as blocked / ready / running, and are managed by the scheduler. They must obey RTOS rules.

**The world of interrupts** is completely different: interrupts are not scheduled, cannot block, do not participate in the RTOS priority system, and may interrupt a task at any moment.

They are **two completely different execution worlds**. The design philosophy of the RTOS is therefore very clear:

> **Interrupts are responsible for "interrupting and notifying"; tasks are responsible for "processing and deciding."**

---

## 2. Why Does an RTOS Limit What Interrupts Can Do?

From an engineering perspective, these limits are not deliberate inconvenience. They come from three very real and unavoidable reasons.

### 1. The Execution Time of an Interrupt Is Uncontrollable

An interrupt may occur between any two instructions, during any task, or even while another interrupt is executing. This means:

> **You can never assume that, when an interrupt occurs, the system is in a safe, stable, and predictable state.**

### 2. There Is No Concept of "Waiting" Inside an Interrupt

In a task, you can call `osDelay()`, wait on a Queue or Semaphore, or voluntarily give up the CPU. In an interrupt, **there is no blocking, no waiting, only "must finish immediately."** Any design that tries to "wait a bit" inside an ISR is fundamentally a logic error.

### 3. Interrupts Cannot Participate in RTOS Scheduling Logic

The core ability of an RTOS is deciding "who runs first" and managing priority relationships between tasks. Interrupts do not participate in priority inheritance and do not obey scheduler rules. This is also why many **priority inversion and timing anomaly problems** eventually surface near ISRs.

---

## 3. The First Iron Rule of RTOS Interrupt Design

If you remember only one sentence from this article, make it this:

> **An interrupt should do only three things: read status, clear flags, and notify a task.**

Any behavior beyond this range should be treated as a **design-level danger signal**.

A healthy ISR usually has these characteristics: extremely short execution time, no business logic, no operation on complex shared resources, and no calls to RTOS APIs that are not `FromISR` variants. It has only one goal:

> **Hand the fact that "an event happened" to an RTOS task as quickly as possible.**

---

## 4. Why Does FreeRTOS Distinguish `FromISR` APIs?

Many people are confused the first time they see these APIs: why design two interfaces for the same function? For example, `xQueueSend` and `xQueueSendFromISR`.

This is not redundant design. It is the engineering boundary written explicitly into the API.

### What Exactly Do `FromISR` APIs Do?

They have only three core characteristics: **never block**, **do not corrupt internal scheduler state**, and **correctly handle whether an immediate task switch is required**. In other words:

> **A `FromISR` API is the only legal channel through which the interrupt world may enter the RTOS kernel.**

### A Common and Fatal Mistake

**Calling a normal RTOS API inside an interrupt**. This kind of mistake usually does not explode on the spot. Instead, it quietly damages system state, leaves some task unable to wake up forever, or crashes during some future scheduling operation. This is one of the **most hidden and hardest-to-reproduce bugs** in RTOS projects.

---

## 5. `configMAX_SYSCALL_INTERRUPT_PRIORITY`: The Invisible Red Line

This is one of the **most easily ignored FreeRTOS configuration items, and one with the most serious engineering consequences**.

### What Does It Actually Limit?

In one sentence: **not every interrupt is "qualified" to call RTOS APIs.** Only interrupts whose priority value is numerically greater than `configMAX_SYSCALL_INTERRUPT_PRIORITY` are allowed to call `FromISR` APIs.

### Why Does This Rule Exist?

Because high-priority interrupts must have **absolute real-time behavior** and must not be affected by internal RTOS mechanisms. The essence of this rule is:

> **The higher the real-time requirement of an interrupt, the less it should directly touch the RTOS kernel.**

### Engineering Consequences

If you call a `FromISR` API inside an interrupt with a priority that is too high, the system behavior becomes **undefined behavior**. It is not guaranteed to fail immediately, but **you can no longer reason about how the system will run.**

---

## 6. Why Does "Doing Too Much in an ISR" Destroy the System?

Many system problems are not caused by code being "wrong." They are caused by **breaking the RTOS's basic assumptions about time and order**.

When you execute complex logic in an interrupt, operate on shared resources, or call many functions, you are lengthening interrupt occupancy, delaying the scheduler, and breaking the real-time assumptions of tasks. The final result is often:

> **The system can still run, but it is no longer stable.**

---

## 7. An Engineering-Level Judgment Standard

When designing or reviewing interrupt code, repeatedly ask yourself three questions:

1. **Must this logic really be completed inside the interrupt?**
2. **What would happen if it were moved into a task?**
3. **Does it touch the scheduling boundary of the RTOS?**

If any of these questions makes you hesitate, that usually means **this code should not remain inside the ISR.**

---

## 8. Put Interrupt Problems Back Into the Overall Philosophy of the RTOS

Returning to the conclusion of the first article: **FreeRTOS is essentially a system of order.** An interrupt is the **only "forced line-cutter" allowed inside that system.**

The RTOS is not trying to eliminate interrupts. It is trying to **restrict them to the smallest, clearest, and most controllable responsibility range.**

---

## Final Words

Many RTOS systems fail not because their task design is not complex enough, but because **interrupts crossed boundaries they should never have crossed**.

Once you begin to respect this boundary, the system's stability, maintainability, and reasonability will change qualitatively.
