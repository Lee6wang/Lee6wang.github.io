## 1. Speed Loop: PI Parameter Tuning Based on Active Damping

For speed-loop PI parameter tuning, the **classical type-II system** in automatic control theory is usually used.

Since some readers may not have studied automatic control theory, first briefly explain what a type-II system is.

### 1.1 From Mechanical Equation to Classical Type-II System

Start from the physical object. The mechanical equation of a PMSM is:

$$
J \frac{d\omega_m}{dt} = T_e - T_L - B\omega_m
\tag{1}
$$

where $J$ is equivalent rotational inertia, $\omega_m$ is mechanical angular speed, $T_e$ is electromagnetic torque, $T_L$ is load torque, and $B$ is the viscous damping coefficient.

If all disturbances except $T_e$ are ignored and the Laplace transform is applied:

$$
\omega(s) = \frac{1}{Js} T_e(s)
\tag{2}
$$

For a PI controller, the speed error is:

$$
e_\omega = \omega^\ast - \omega
\tag{3}
$$

The PI controller is:

$$
i_q^\ast(s) = K_p e_\omega(s) + \frac{K_i}{s} e_\omega(s)
\tag{4}
$$

Multiplying equations (2) and (4) gives the open-loop transfer function:

$$
L(s) = \frac{K_t (K_p s + K_i)}{J s^2}
\tag{5}
$$

This is the **classical type-II system** of the speed loop.

### 1.2 Simplification of the Torque Equation

Now return to the main topic. To make speed-loop PI tuning easier, rewrite the torque equation of the three-phase PMSM:

$$
T_e = \frac{3}{2} p_n i_q \left[ i_d (L_d - L_q) + \psi_f \right]
\tag{6}
$$

When the $i_d=0$ control strategy is adopted, it simplifies to:

$$
T_e = K_t i_q
\tag{7}
$$

where \(K_t = \frac{3}{2} p_n \psi_f\).

### 1.3 Introduction and Physical Meaning of Active Damping

According to the reference mentioned in Yuan Lei's book, *Torque-maximizing field-weakening control: design, analysis and parameter selection*, the concept of "active damping" can be used to design the speed-loop PI regulator:

$$
i_q = i_q' - B_a \omega_m
\tag{8}
$$

Here $i_q$ is the q-axis current actually applied to the motor, $i_q'$ is the current command originally calculated by the PI speed controller, and $B_a$ is an artificially designed "active damping coefficient".

To make this easier to understand, substitute equation (8) into equation (7):

$$
T_e = K_t (i_q' - B_a \omega_m) = K_t i_q' - K_t B_a \omega_m
\tag{9}
$$

Put it back into the mechanical equation:

$$
J \dot{\omega}_m = K_t i_q' - T_L - (B + K_t B_a) \omega_m
\tag{10}
$$

Compared with equation (1), a new conclusion can be obtained:

$$
B_{\text{equivalent}} = B + K_t B_a
\tag{11}
$$

This term means the algorithm has artificially "created a damping term". This damping is implemented through active power, meaning current doing work, so it is called **active damping**.

**Why add active damping?**

**Reason 1: provide the missing energy-dissipation channel for the speed loop**

- Traditional speed loop: mainly relies on PI zero placement to shape damping, while the plant itself almost does not dissipate energy.
- After adding active damping: the system can actively "brake" at the motion level, so energy is not fully pushed onto PI tuning.

**Reason 2: decouple damping design from PI parameters**

- In traditional PI, $K_p$ and $K_i$ affect damping, response speed, and steady-state error at the same time.
- After adding active damping, $B_a$ mainly determines damping/overshoot, while PI mainly handles bandwidth and steady-state accuracy.

### 1.4 Derivation of the Transfer Function

Now return to the formulas. Under the $i_d=0$ strategy and assuming the motor starts with no load, \(T_L=0\), equations (1), (6), and (8) give:

$$
\frac{d\omega_m}{dt} = \frac{1.5 p_n \psi_f}{J}(i_q' - B_a \omega_m) - \frac{B}{J} \omega_m
\tag{12}
$$

The damping terms can be merged:

$$
\frac{d\omega_m}{dt} = \frac{1.5 p_n \psi_f}{J} i_q' - \underbrace{\frac{B + 1.5 p_n \psi_f B_a}{J}}_{\text{equivalent damping}} \omega_m
\tag{13}
$$

Applying the Laplace transform to equation (13):

$$
Js \omega_m(s) + (B + 1.5 p_n \psi_f B_a) \omega_m(s) = 1.5 p_n \psi_f i_q'(s)
\tag{14}
$$

Rearranging gives the standard form:

$$
\omega_m(s) = \frac{\dfrac{1.5 p_n \psi_f}{J}}{s + \dfrac{B + 1.5 p_n \psi_f B_a}{J}} i_q'(s)
\tag{15}
$$

If the pole is expected to be $-\beta$, then:

$$
\frac{B + 1.5 p_n \psi_f B_a}{J} = \beta
\tag{16}
$$

Thus:

$$
B_a = \frac{\beta J - B}{1.5 p_n \psi_f}
\tag{17}
$$

### 1.5 Closed-Loop System Design

According to the traditional PI regulator, the speed-loop controller expression is:

$$
i_q^\ast = \left( K_{p\omega} + \frac{K_{i\omega}}{s} \right) \underbrace{(\omega_m^\ast - \omega_m)}_{\text{speed error}} - B_a \omega_m
\tag{18}
$$

Assume the current loop is fast enough, so \(i_q = i_q^\ast\). Combining with equation (8):

$$
i_q' = i_q + B_a \omega_m = i_q^\ast + B_a \omega_m
\tag{19}
$$

Substitute equation (18):

$$
i_q' = \left( K_{p\omega} + \frac{K_{i\omega}}{s} \right) (\omega_m^\ast - \omega_m)
\tag{20}
$$

From equation (15), the transfer function is:

$$
\frac{\omega_m(s)}{i_q'(s)} = \frac{\dfrac{1.5 p_n \psi_f}{J}}{s + \beta}
\tag{21}
$$

For simpler notation, define:

$$
K_m = \frac{1.5 p_n \psi_f}{J}
\tag{22}
$$

Then:

$$
\frac{\omega_m(s)}{i_q'(s)} = \frac{K_m}{s + \beta}
\tag{23}
$$

Rewrite equation (20):

$$
i_q' = \left( \frac{K_{p\omega} s + K_{i\omega}}{s} \right) (\omega_m^\ast - \omega_m)
\tag{24}
$$

The system structure can be represented as:

![Speed-loop structure](../assets/figure/2026-01-28/2.png)

From equations (23) and (24):

**PI controller:**

$$
G_c(s) = \frac{K_{p\omega} s + K_{i\omega}}{s}
\tag{25}
$$

**Controlled plant:**

$$
G_p(s) = \frac{K_m}{s + \beta}
\tag{26}
$$

The closed-loop transfer function is:

$$
\frac{\omega_m(s)}{\omega_m^\ast(s)} = \frac{G_c(s) G_p(s)}{1 + G_c(s) G_p(s)}
\tag{27}
$$

After calculation and simplification:

$$
\frac{\omega_m(s)}{\omega_m^\ast(s)} = \frac{K_m (K_{p\omega} s + K_{i\omega})}{s^2 + (\beta + K_m K_{p\omega}) s + K_m K_{i\omega}}
\tag{28}
$$

### 1.6 Zero-Pole Cancellation and Parameter Calculation

To make the system behave as a first-order system with bandwidth \(\beta\):

$$
\frac{\omega_m(s)}{\omega_m^\ast(s)} = \frac{\beta}{s + \beta}
\tag{29}
$$

Choose **zero-pole cancellation**, so the zero in the numerator cancels one pole in the denominator and the system becomes first order.

Let the denominator factorize as:

$$
s^2 + (\beta + K_m K_{p\omega}) s + K_m K_{i\omega} = (s + \beta)(s + K_m K_{p\omega})
\tag{30}
$$

Expanding the right side:

$$
(s + \beta)(s + K_m K_{p\omega}) = s^2 + (\beta + K_m K_{p\omega}) s + \beta K_m K_{p\omega}
\tag{31}
$$

Comparing constant terms:

$$
K_{i\omega} = \beta K_{p\omega}
\tag{32}
$$

**Verify zero-pole cancellation:**

The numerator is:

$$
K_m K_{p\omega} s + K_m K_{i\omega} = K_m K_{p\omega} \left( s + \frac{K_{i\omega}}{K_{p\omega}} \right) = K_m K_{p\omega} (s + \beta)
\tag{33}
$$

The denominator is:

$$
(s + \beta)(s + K_m K_{p\omega})
\tag{34}
$$

Therefore:

$$
\frac{\omega_m(s)}{\omega_m^\ast(s)} = \frac{K_m K_{p\omega} (s + \beta)}{(s + \beta)(s + K_m K_{p\omega})} = \frac{K_m K_{p\omega}}{s + K_m K_{p\omega}}
\tag{35}
$$

We want the closed-loop bandwidth to be $\beta$, so:

$$
K_m K_{p\omega} = \beta
\tag{36}
$$

**Final parameter formulas:**

Calculate $K_{p\omega}$:

$$
K_{p\omega} = \frac{\beta}{K_m} = \frac{\beta}{\dfrac{1.5 p_n \psi_f}{J}} = \frac{\beta J}{1.5 p_n \psi_f}
\tag{37}
$$

Calculate $K_{i\omega}$:

$$
K_{i\omega} = \beta K_{p\omega} = \frac{\beta^2 J}{1.5 p_n \psi_f}
\tag{38}
$$

## 2. Current Loop: PI Parameter Tuning Based on Internal Model Control

### 2.1 Current Equations in the d-q Coordinate System

For controller design, rewrite the current equations in the $d$-$q$ coordinate system:

$$
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{d i_d}{dt} = -\dfrac{R}{L_d} i_d + \dfrac{L_q}{L_d} \omega_e i_q + \dfrac{1}{L_d} u_d \\[8pt]
\dfrac{d i_q}{dt} = -\dfrac{R}{L_q} i_q - \dfrac{1}{L_q} \omega_e (L_d i_d + \psi_f) + \dfrac{1}{L_q} u_q
\end{cases}
\tag{39}
$$

From equation (39), stator currents $i_d$ and $i_q$ generate **cross-coupled electromotive forces** in the d-axis and q-axis directions. This is very difficult for control.

### 2.2 Voltage Equations and Coupling-Term Analysis

For easier memorization, first give the formulas of $u_d$ and $u_q$:

$$
\begin{cases}
u_d = R i_d + L_d \dfrac{d i_d}{dt} - \omega_e L_q i_q \\[10pt]
u_q = R i_q + L_q \dfrac{d i_q}{dt} + \omega_e (L_d i_d + \psi_f)
\end{cases}
\tag{40}
$$

The physical meanings of the coupling terms are:

> - \(\omega_e L_q i_q\): induced electromotive force generated by q-axis current during rotation, affecting the d-axis.
> - \(\omega_e (L_d i_d + \psi_f)\): back EMF generated by d-axis flux linkage during rotation, affecting the q-axis.

These coupling terms make the d-axis and q-axis influence each other, so they cannot be controlled independently.

### 2.3 Feedforward Decoupling and Its Limitations

If the coupling is fully canceled by adding compensation terms, equation (40) becomes:

$$
\begin{cases}
u_{d0} = u_d + \omega_e L_q i_q = R i_d + L_d \dfrac{d i_d}{dt} \\[8pt]
u_{q0} = u_q - \omega_e (L_d i_d + \psi_f) = R i_q + L_q \dfrac{d i_q}{dt}
\end{cases}
\tag{41}
$$

Here $u_{d0}$ and $u_{q0}$ are the decoupled d-axis and q-axis voltages.

After decoupling, each axis becomes an independent first-order RL circuit and can be designed independently.

Applying the Laplace transform to equation (41):

$$
Y(s) = G(s) U(s)
\tag{42}
$$

where:

$$
U(s) = \begin{bmatrix} u_{d0}(s) \\ u_{q0}(s) \end{bmatrix}, \quad
Y(s) = \begin{bmatrix} i_d(s) \\ i_q(s) \end{bmatrix}, \quad
G(s) = \begin{bmatrix} R + sL_d & 0 \\ 0 & R + sL_q \end{bmatrix}^{-1}
$$

Here \(G(s)\) is a diagonal matrix, showing that the \(d\)-\(q\) axes have been fully decoupled. The single-axis transfer functions are:

$$
\begin{cases}
G_d(s) = \dfrac{I_d(s)}{U_{d0}(s)} = \dfrac{1}{R + sL_d} = \dfrac{1/R}{1 + s\tau_d} \\[10pt]
G_q(s) = \dfrac{I_q(s)}{U_{q0}(s)} = \dfrac{1}{R + sL_q} = \dfrac{1/R}{1 + s\tau_q}
\end{cases}
\tag{43}
$$

where \(\tau_d = L_d/R\) and \(\tau_q = L_q/R\) are electrical time constants.

Using regular PI regulators together with feedforward decoupling, the d-q voltage commands are:

$$
\begin{cases}
u_d^\ast = \underbrace{\left( K_{pd} + \dfrac{K_{id}}{s} \right) (i_d^\ast - i_d)}_{\text{PI controller}} - \underbrace{\omega_e L_q i_q}_{\text{feedforward decoupling}} \\[12pt]
u_q^\ast = \underbrace{\left( K_{pq} + \dfrac{K_{iq}}{s} \right) (i_q^\ast - i_q)}_{\text{PI controller}} + \underbrace{\omega_e (L_d i_d + \psi_f)}_{\text{feedforward decoupling}}
\end{cases}
\tag{44}
$$

**Limitations of feedforward decoupling:**

If only feedforward decoupling is used, several problems remain:

- The decoupling terms depend on motor parameters.
- Complete decoupling is achieved only when model parameters exactly match the real parameters.
- In real systems, especially interior PMSM, magnetic saturation and temperature variation change parameters.
- When parameters mismatch, decoupling becomes incomplete and control performance decreases.

Therefore, **Internal Model Control (IMC)** is introduced here.

### 2.4 Principle of Internal Model Control

For IMC, first use an analogy:

> Imagine driving a car and wanting to maintain a constant speed.
>
> **Traditional feedback control:**
> - You look at the speedometer. If the speed is low, you press the accelerator; if it is high, you release it.
> - The adjustment is based only on "error".
>
> **Internal model control:**
> - You have a "vehicle model" in your mind and know how much acceleration a certain throttle amount will produce.
> - You first calculate the required throttle based on the model.
> - Then you observe the difference between actual speed and model prediction and use that difference for correction.

The core of internal model control is to use the "knowledge" of the controlled object, namely its model, to improve control performance.

**Characteristics of IMC:**

- Low requirement for model precision
- Not sensitive to parameter variation, meaning good robustness
- Simple structure with only one adjustable parameter \(\alpha\)
- Convenient for online calculation

**Basic structure of IMC:**

![Internal model control structure](../assets/figure/2026-01-28/3.png)

Here \(\hat{G}(s)\) is the internal model, \(G(s)\) is the controlled plant, \(C(s)\) is the internal-model controller, \(U(s)\) is the controller output such as voltage, and \(Y(s)\) is the system output.

**Two extreme cases:**

**Case 1: the model is completely accurate**

In this case, the controller input becomes the signal itself, and the feedback channel remains zero. The closed-loop transfer function becomes:

$$
\frac{Y(s)}{R(s)} = G(s) C(s)
\tag{45}
$$

This is equivalent to a complete open-loop control, where the output is fully determined by \(G(s)C(s)\).

**Case 2: the model is completely inaccurate**

If the internal model is completely wrong, for example \(\hat{G}(s)=0\), the feedback signal is not changed in any way, and the system becomes a traditional negative-feedback control system.

In general, the system model always contains some error. IMC can use this error for correction, combining the advantages of feedforward and feedback.

### 2.5 Design of the Internal Model Controller

The block diagram above can be equivalently transformed into:

![IMC equivalent controller](../assets/figure/2026-01-28/4.png)

The equivalent controller is:

$$
F(s) = \left[ I - C(s) G(s) \right]^{-1} C(s)
\tag{46}
$$

If the desired closed-loop transfer function is \(G_c(s)\):

$$
G_c(s) = G(s) C(s)
\tag{47}
$$

then:

$$
C(s) = G^{-1} G_c(s)
\tag{48}
$$

However, the inverse may not exist. If it is nonproper, meaning the numerator order is greater than the denominator order, it is physically unrealizable and may also introduce instability.

**Introduce a low-pass filter:**

To make the controller realizable, introduce the low-pass filter \(L(s)\):

$$
C(s) = \hat{G}^{-1}(s) L(s)
\tag{49}
$$

where \(L(s) = \dfrac{\alpha}{s + \alpha}\).

Its main functions are:

- Make the controller proper and realizable.
- Limit high-frequency gain and reduce noise influence.
- Provide one adjustable parameter \(\alpha\).

When \(\hat{G}^{-1} = G(s)\):

$$
G_c(s) = G(s) C(s) = G(s) G^{-1}(s) L(s) = L(s) = \frac{\alpha}{s + \alpha}
\tag{50}
$$

The closed-loop system becomes first order.

### 2.6 Current-Loop PI Parameter Derivation

Return to equation (43). The internal-model controller for the d-axis is:

$$
C(s) = \hat{G}^{-1}(s) L(s) = (R + sL_d) \frac{\alpha}{s + \alpha}
\tag{51}
$$

Substitute it back into equation (46):

$$
C(s) \hat{G}(s) = (R + sL_d) \cdot \frac{\alpha}{s + \alpha} \cdot \frac{1}{R + sL_d} = \frac{\alpha}{s + \alpha}
\tag{52}
$$

$$
I - C(s) \hat{G}(s) = 1 - \frac{\alpha}{s + \alpha} = \frac{s}{s + \alpha}
\tag{53}
$$

$$
\left[ I - C(s) \hat{G}(s) \right]^{-1} = \frac{s + \alpha}{s}
\tag{54}
$$

$$
F(s) = \frac{s + \alpha}{s} \cdot (R + sL_d) \cdot \frac{\alpha}{s + \alpha} = \frac{\alpha(R + sL_d)}{s}
\tag{55}
$$

$$
F(s) = \alpha L_d + \frac{\alpha R}{s} = K_p + \frac{K_i}{s}
\tag{56}
$$

Similarly, the corresponding \(K_p\) and \(K_i\) of the q-axis are:

$$
\begin{cases}
K_{pd} = \alpha L_d \\
K_{id} = \alpha R \\
K_{pq} = \alpha L_q \\
K_{iq} = \alpha R
\end{cases}
\tag{57}
$$

### 2.7 Constraint on the Bandwidth Parameter Alpha

It may look like \(\alpha\) is excellent and increasing it always improves performance. But in reality, it cannot be increased indefinitely.

The electrical time constant \(T_d = L_d/R\) means:

- Under constant voltage, the current needs this time constant to rise from 0 to 63.2% of its steady-state value.
- This is an inherent characteristic of the motor and cannot be changed by the controller.

Clearly, \(\alpha\) cannot exceed the current change rate determined by the time constant. Therefore, the constraint is:

$$
\alpha < \frac{1}{T_d} = \frac{R}{L_d}
\tag{58}
$$

## 3. Summary

After the PI current loop and speed loop are designed, vector control finally has a solid foundation. But in real engineering, this control structure based on an ideal model will still face challenges: parameter drift, magnetic saturation, inverter nonlinearity, sampling delay, and computational delay all gradually appear under higher speed, heavier load, and more complex operating conditions.
