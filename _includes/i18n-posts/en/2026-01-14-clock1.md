# The Design Philosophy and Engineering Consequences of the STM32 Clock Architecture

**From "frequency configuration" to "system behavior constraints".**

## Foreword

The recent school exams are finally over, so I can take some time again. While reading and writing code recently, I often ran into STM32 clock-configuration issues. From the earliest stage of learning microcontrollers, many of us probably started from 51 or 32 courses step by step. But introductory courses often only teach how to configure the clock and how to make it run at the highest frequency. They rarely explain why it should be configured that way or why the architecture is designed this way.

Today, let's talk about the design philosophy of the STM32 clock architecture, and the problems and solutions that may appear in engineering practice.

## Introduction: Why Do STM32 Clock Problems Usually Appear Only in Mass Production and Extreme Conditions?

In STM32 projects, **90% of clock configurations can "run" in the lab**, but the real problems often appear under:

- High temperature / low temperature
- EMI environments
- Long-time operation
- Bootloader / App switching
- Mass-production batch variation

The reason is not complicated:

**The clock system has never been only a question of "whether the frequency is correct", but a question of "whether system behavior is correctly constrained".**

The clock architecture of STM32, especially the F1 series, is essentially a **system-engineering product that balances cost, reliability, and verification complexity**.

## 1. Single PLL + Multiple Dividers: The Core Design Choice of the F1 Clock Architecture

### 1.1 Why Does F1 Not Use Multiple PLLs?

**Conclusion first:** F1 uses a "single PLL + AHB/APB divider tree" not because of lack of capability, but because it **deliberately compresses analog uncertainty**.

#### The Real Structural Reason

- **PLL is an analog block, not a free resource.**
  - Each additional PLL adds:
    - VCO
    - Loop filter
    - Lock-state machine
    - PVT corner verification space
- **The design goal of F1 is not "independent multi-domain clocks", but "coverage of mainstream scenarios".**
  - 72 MHz CPU
  - 36 MHz APB1
  - 48 MHz derived for USB
  - A single PLL is enough for these requirements.

> In other words:  
> **F1 chooses to concentrate all uncertainty in one place instead of spreading it across multiple PLLs.**

#### Essential Difference from F4 / F7 / H7

- **F1:** centralized clock risk, managed by dividers
- **F4/F7:** multi-output PLLs serving rigid domains such as USB and SDIO
- **H7:** lightly SoC-like, with multiple clock domains and multiple power domains working together

### 1.2 When Must You Be Aware of the Cost of a Single PLL?

When any of the following appears:

- The system contains at the same time:
  - USB, with rigid 48 MHz requirement
  - High-precision PWM / control loop
  - UART / CAN communication
- The system is sensitive to **short-term stability, meaning jitter**
- Long-term stability is required under **high temperature / low temperature / EMI**

Then you must clearly understand one thing:

> **In a single-PLL architecture, any input choice will be "broadcast" to the whole system.**

### 1.3 Typical Engineering Consequences of Not Understanding This

- UART:
  - OK in the lab, but occasional framing errors on-site
- CAN:
  - Error counter slowly rises and finally reaches error passive
- Control system:
  - PWM edge jitter -> sampling phase jitter -> current spikes
- USB:
  - SYSCLK looks correct, but enumeration occasionally fails

The common feature of these problems is:

> **The system does not die immediately; instead, its behavior becomes unpredictable.**

### 1.4 Engineering Judgment Criteria

- **Design PLL input and multiplication around rigid clock domains first.**
- CPU frequency is not the target; it is the result of constraints.
- In a single-PLL system:
  - "Runs faster" does not mean "system is stronger".
  - In many cases, the opposite is true.

## 2. APB1 Frequency Limit: Reliability Design, Not a Performance Bottleneck

### 2.1 Why Is APB1 Limited to 36 MHz?

**Conclusion:** APB1 carries a group of peripherals with the most conservative timing paths and the highest system risk.

Typical examples include:

- I2C
- CAN
- USART2 / USART3
- PWR / BKP
- Some general-purpose timers

These modules share three features:

1. Mixed digital and analog behavior
2. Long external connections and complex environments
3. Higher sensitivity to timing boundaries

### 2.2 Engineering Scenarios That Must Be Watched

- SYSCLK is pulled to the upper limit, such as 72 MHz.
- CAN / I2C is used in:
  - Automotive
  - Industrial
  - Long-cable
  - Strong-interference environments

At this point, **whether PCLK1 is within specification directly determines whether the system can be mass produced.**

### 2.3 Real Consequences of Ignoring the APB1 Limit

- I2C:
  - Occasional NACK
  - Bus lock-up, not always reproducible
- CAN:
  - Error counter rises
  - Random bus-off
- USART:
  - Occasional errors at certain baud rates

These problems are **almost impossible to solve completely through software retry alone**.

### 2.4 Engineering Judgment Criteria

- **Always leave margin for PCLK1.**
- For field systems:
  - Lowering PCLK1 is often more effective than adding delay, retries, or watchdog logic.

### 2.5 Verification Methods

- Derive PCLK1 backward from RCC configuration.
- Compare with peripheral registers to calculate real timing:
  - CAN BTR
  - I2C CCR
  - USART BRR
- Stress test with:
  - Temperature
  - Power-supply ripple
  - Cable length

## 3. Timer Clock Doubling Rule: A Hardware Compensation Mechanism That Is Easy to Miss

### 3.1 Why Is TIMCLK = 2 x PCLK When the APB Prescaler Is Not 1?

**One-sentence explanation:** this prevents APB division from directly destroying timer resolution.

Without the doubling mechanism:

- PWM precision decreases
- Capture resolution decreases
- Control performance directly worsens

### 3.2 When Will This Cause Engineering Mistakes?

When you:

- Divide APB1 to meet the specification
- Use timers for:
  - PWM
  - Input capture
  - Encoder
  - Frequency measurement

If PSC / ARR are calculated only from PCLK, **they will be wrong**.

### 3.3 Hidden Consequences of Ignoring the Doubling Rule

- Surface problem: frequency is wrong by a factor of two.
- Deeper problem:
  - Control period is silently changed.
  - System performance becomes worse even though the logic is completely "correct".

This is a typical **slow engineering bug**.

### 3.4 Engineering Judgment Criteria

- **TIMCLK must be calculated explicitly.**
- The project should contain a unified function that:
  - Reads RCC divider configuration
  - Dynamically derives the timer base frequency

## 4. The Real Design Goal of RCC: Safety First, Not Performance First

### 4.1 RCC Is Not a "Configuration Module"; It Is a "Risk-Management Module"

The design focus of RCC is:

- **Step-by-step switching**
- **Detectable state**
- **Failure fallback**

This is why:

- Ready flags exist
- CSS exists
- Booting from HSI is recommended

### 4.2 The Most Dangerous Misunderstanding in Engineering

> **PLLRDY = electrical stability**

This is wrong.

- PLLRDY only means:
  - Digital lock has completed.
- It does not guarantee:
  - Phase noise has converged.
  - Power supply is stable.
  - Jitter is within an acceptable range.

### 4.3 Typical Consequences of Ignoring the Stabilization Window

- USB initialization occasionally fails.
- High-precision peripherals occasionally behave abnormally.
- The system does not die, but the time base has drifted.

### 4.4 Engineering Judgment Criteria

Clock switching should follow this order:

```text
HSI -> HSE -> PLL
-> SYSCLK switch
-> stabilization wait
-> rigid peripheral initialization
-> software time-base synchronization
```

Also:

- **Any external clock must have a timeout and degradation path.**

### 4.5 Verification Methods

- Intentionally create failures:
  - Remove the crystal
  - Lower the voltage
  - Start at low temperature
- Observe switching transients through MCO.
- Toggle GPIO to measure the stabilization window.

## Conclusion: Engineering-Level Conclusions for the STM32 Clock System

### The Five Most Common Misunderstandings

1. The clock is not "frequency"; it is "system constraints".
2. A single PLL broadcasts error and jitter.
3. APB1 frequency limiting is reliability design.
4. The TIM doubling rule is not a detail; it is essential.
5. Ready does not equal stable; switching must leave a window.

### Summary

> **A good STM32 clock design is not "it can run at 72 MHz".  
> It is that the system behaves consistently under any temperature, power, load, and time scale.**
